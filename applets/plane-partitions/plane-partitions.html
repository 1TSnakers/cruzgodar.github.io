!header Plane Partitions !begin-text-block This is a behemoth of an applet — it took two months of work, I had to learn how to use a proper 3D library, and there’s more than twice as much code as the previous largest applet. I’m proud of the result, though: this applet visualizes plane partitions, which are stacks of boxes with one restriction: the height doesn’t increase when moving down or to the right. The resulting object can be viewed in 2D or 3D — the 3D view looks like a bunch of rhombi, and drawing a line in the center (called a dimer) produces a one-to-one correspondence between plane partitions and what are called perfect matchings on a hexagonal lattice, where every node is pared with exactly one other. Plane partitions can be made asymptotic to a shape. When adding or editing an array, replace a number with a caret (^) to give that square infinite height, represented with slightly lighter boxes. Plane partitions are a frequently studied object, and a handful of correspondences involving them have been discovered. Three of them have been implemented here, under the Algorithms section. All three take a plane partition and convert it to a tableau of the same shape: a tableau is a collection of numbers with no restriction on box heights decreasing in any direction. The inverse algorithms take a tableau and convert it back to a plane partition. Pak and Sulzgruber are independently discovered correspondences with wildly different descriptions, but amazingly produce the same results, while Hillman-Grassl is a unique correspondence all its own. !end-text-block
