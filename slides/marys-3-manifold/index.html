Mary’s Room

Australian philosopher Frank Jackson proposed the thought experiment of a scientist who is devoted to learning everything there is to know about color, despite being stuck in a strictly black-and-white room.

What’s a Geometry, Anyway?

A geometry on a smooth manifold $X$ is a maximal Lie group $G$ acting transitively on $X$ with compact point stabilizers, which implies the existence of a $G$-invariant Riemannian metric.

The uniformization theorem classifies simply connected Riemannian 2-manifolds into three classes up to conformal (angle-preserving) mapping: the sphere, the torus, and the open disk.

Since the metric on a Riemannian manifold induces a metric on its universal cover, geometries on Riemannian manifolds are classified by the induced geometry on the universal cover.

By simulating a scene with light traveling along geodesics, we can see what it’s like to live in a 2-manifold like the sphere.

The rendered image is 1-dimensional, with each point corresponding to a unit vector in the tangent plane to the sphere at the camera’s position.

What About 3-Manifolds?

Thurston’s geometrization conjecture is an analogue of the uniformization theorem for oriented and closed 3-manifolds: those that are compact and have no boundary.

Unlike with 2-manifolds, not every closed 3-manifolds is differmorphic to one a single geometric structure. Instead, they decompose as connected sums of prime 3-manifolds — those that are not a nontrivial connected sum.

Prime 3-manifolds have 8 possible geometric structures: three direct analogues of the 2-dimensional ones, two product geometries, and three new ones that are substantially stranger.
