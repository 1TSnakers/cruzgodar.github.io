!header Section 6: The Ratio and Root Tests
!begin-text-block Our final two tests for convergence have the nice effect of applying to series with positive and negative terms alike. They’re simple to state and apply, and together with the previous five tests, they round out a solid theory of convergence. The Harmonic series diverges because even through its terms shrink to zero, they don’t do so fast enough. So far, we haven’t had a way to measure what “fast enough” means exactly, but our final two tests will each provide a possible interpretation. One idea we might pursue is looking at the size of terms not only in an absolute sense, but relative to each other as well. For example, when $n$ is very large, the two successive terms $\frac{1}{n}$ and $\frac{1}{n + 1}$ in the harmonic series are very nearly the same size. The ratio between them is $$ \frac{\frac{1}{n + 1}}{\frac{1}{n}} = \frac{n + 1}{n} \to 1 $$ as $n \to \infty$. On the other hand, something like the geometric series $\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{1}{2^n}$ has a ratio of successive terms equal to $$ \frac{\frac{1}{2^{n + 1}}}{\frac{1}{2^n} = \frac{2^n}{2^{n + 1}} = \frac{1}{2}. $$ In other words, not only are the terms of that geometric series getting smaller in an absolute sense (i.e. approaching zero), they’re also shrinking relative to each other, whereas the terms of the Harmonic series are not consistently smaller relative to each other for large $n$. This idea of quantifying the ratio between successive terms is the centeral mechanic of our first test. !end-text-block !begin-thm the ratio test Let $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n$ be a series and define a value $r$ by $$ r = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n} \right|. $$ If $0 \leq r < 1$, then the series converges. If $r > 1$, the series diverges. If $r = 1$, the test is inconclusive. !end-thm !begin-text-block It’s worth briefly justifying why the ratio test works. If $r > 1$, then eventually every term is larger than the previous, so the divergence test tells us the series must diverge. If $r < 1$, then there is some number $s$ with $r < s < 1$ so that for large enough $n$ (say all $n \geq N$), $a_{n + 1} < s a_n$. Then $a_{n + 2} < s a_{n + 1} < s^2 a_n$, and so on. This looks a whole lot like a geometric series, and what we end up with is $$ \begin{align*} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n &< \sum_{n = 1}^{N - 1} a_n + a_N \sum_{n = 0}^\infty s^n\\ &< \sum_{n = 1}^{N - 1} a_n + a_N \cdot \frac{1}{1 - s}, \end{align*} $$ which is finite. This is a bit grittier than our justifications usually are, and it’s perfectly fine to engage with the ratio test at the level of just remembering the theorem statement, but under the hood, it’s all just geometric series. The ratio test, like every other test we know of, is incapable of determining what value a convergent series converges to. It can function on series with negative terms, though, making it fairly versatile. !end-text-block !begin-example the ratio test Determine if the series $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{e^n}{n!}$ converges or diverges. We’ll start, as always, with the divergence test. As $n \to \infty$, the behavior of $\frac{e^n}{n!}$ is a little complicated. Which grows faster: an exponential, or a factorial? Since the factorial function is defined only on positive integers, there’s no equivalent function we know on real numbers that we can differentiate in order to apply L’Hôpital — we just have to reason this one out. Both $e^n$ and $n!$ are products of $n$ numbers: we have $$ \frac{e^n}{n!} = \frac{e \cdot e \cdots e \cdot e}{n \cdot (n-1) \cdots 2 \cdot 1}. $$ Whenever $n$ increases by $1$, the numerator is increased by a factor of $e$, while the denominator is increased by a factor of $n$. In total, that means multiplying by $\frac{e}{n}$, and since $n \to \infty$ and $e$ is a constant, we’re multiplying by smaller and smaller numbers every time. In total, $\frac{e^n}{n!} \to 0$. Therefore, the divergence test is inconclusive. In general, the ratio test works best with series whose terms have lots of exponentials and/or factorials — this has plenty of both, so let’s give it a shot. We have $$ \begin{align*} r &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{\frac{e^{n + 1}}{(n + 1)!}}{\frac{e^n}{n!}} \right|\\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{(n!)(e^{n + 1})}{((n + 1)!)(e^n)} \right|\\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{(n!)(e)}{(n + 1)!} \right|\\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{e \cdot n \cdot (n - 1) \cdots 2 \cdot 1}{(n + 1) \cdot n \cdot (n - 1) \cdots 2 \cdot 1} \right|\\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{e}{n + 1} \right|\\ &= 0. \end{align*} $$ By the ratio test, the series converges. !end-example !begin-exercise the ratio test Determine if the following series converge or diverge. 1. $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n n}{2^n}$. 2. $\sum_{k = 1}^\infty \frac{(k!)^2}{(2k)!}$. !end-exercise !begin-exercise the ratio test Let’s show that when $r = 1$ in the ratio test, we really can’t determine anything about the series. Of the three following series, one is divergent, one is absolutely convergent, and one is conditionally convergent. Determine which is which, and then show that $r = 1$ for all three. 1. $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n}$. 2. $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$. 3. $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty (-1)^n$. !end-exercise !nav-buttons !footer
