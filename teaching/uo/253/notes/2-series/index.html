!header Section 2: Series
!begin-text-block With a strong foundation of sequences built up, let’s turn to their main application in this course: infinite series. Where a sequence is an infinite list of numbers, a series is an infinite sum. Keep in mind the course roadmap: eventually, we’ll want to add up not just numbers, but monomials, forming an object that’s something like a polynomial with infinitely many terms. To start with, though, we’ll stick to numbers only. !end-text-block !begin-def series A series is a sum of the terms in a sequence $(a_n)$, written $$ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n = a_1 + a_2 + a_3 + \cdots. $$ !end-def !begin-text-block The zig-zag shape is the Greek letter Sigma — S for sum. The expressions above and below it don’t always have to be $n = 1$ and $\infty$: the bottom expression indicates the index variable (here, that’s $n$) and the first value it should take (1), and the top number is the last value it should take (here, the $\infty$ means to keep going and never stop). Let’s look at some examples of series to get a sense of how they can differ. !end-text-block !begin-example series Evaluate the following sums. 1. $\sum_{n = 1}^5 n$. 2. $\sum_{i = -2}^2 \frac{1}{i^2 + 1}$. 3. $\sum_{k = 0}^\infty 2$. 1. Expanding out the first sum, we have $$ \sum_{n = 1}^5 n = 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 = 15. $$ If you’ve done any programming before, series look a whole lot like for loops. If you get into coding in the future, for loops will look a lot like series. The idea is very similar to a sequence, except that we don’t just write out all the terms — we add them up, one by one. 2. This time, the index variable is $i$, not $n$. We start it at $-2$, increase it by $1$ until it hits $2$, and add up every term we get along the way. That comes out to $$ \begin{align*} \sum_{i = -2}^2 \frac{1}{i^2 + 1} &= \frac{1}{5} + \frac{1}{2} + 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{5}\\ &= \frac{12}{5}. \end{align*} $$ 3. This one is a little weird. Our index variable is $k$, but it doesn’t appear anywhere in the terms we’re adding up. Instead, we’re supposed to just add up a bunch of $2$s as $k$ increases. Since there are infinitely many values for $k$ to take, there are infinitely many $2$s to add up. So we get $$ \sum_{k = 0}^\infty 2 = 2 + 2 + 2 + \cdots. $$ It’s pretty clear that this won’t have any meaningful value, but we don’t yet have the language to describe how or why. With that in mind, let’s talk about what it means for a series to converge. !end-example !begin-def partial sums Let $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n$ be an infinite series. The $k$th partial sum, written $S_k$, is defined as $$ S_k = \sum_{n = 1}^k a_n. $$ In other words, the $k$th partial sum of a series is obtained by adding up its first $k$ terms. !end-def !begin-exercise partial sums For each of the following series, write out the first five terms. Then find the first, third, and fifth partial sums. 1. $\sum_{n = 0}^\infty n$. 2. $\sum_{m = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{m}$. 3. $\sum_{k = 1}^\infty \frac{2}{3^k}$. !end-exercise !begin-text-block It’s common to confuse the terms of a series with its partial sums — hopefully this helps disambiguate them. The purpose of partial sums is to let us talk about the convergence of series. The behavior of the terms turns out not to be particularly helpful: if we want to assign a value to $\sum_{m = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{m}$, then knowing that $\left( \frac{1}{m} \right) \to 0$ doesn’t tell us much. In fact, if we’re adding up infinitely many positive things, those things had better converge to zero — if they don’t, there’s no chance the sum will be finite. What we want is the limit of a sequence that gives better and better approximations of the infinite sum, and that’s exactly what the sequence of partial sums does. !end-text-block !begin-def convergence of series Let $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n$ be an infinite series. If the sequence $(S_k)$ of partial sums converges to a limit $S$, then we say that the series converges to $S$, and write $$ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n = S. $$ If $(S_k)$ diverges, then we say that the series diverges. !end-def !begin-text-block Notice the difference in notation between sequences and series: if a sequence $(a_n)$ converges to $a$, we write $(a_n) \to a$, with an arrow marking convergence. On the other hand, a convergent series is written $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty b_n = b$, with an equals sign. This is because a convergent series is equal to its limit, whereas a convergent series is still just a list of numbers. !end-text-block !begin-example convergence of a series Evaluate $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^n}$ or show it diverges. We start by finding the partial sums. The first few are $$ \begin{align*} S_1 &= \frac{1}{2}\\ S_2 &= \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4}\\ S_3 &= \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{8} = \frac{7}{8}\\ S_4 &= \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{16} = \frac{15}{16}\\ \end{align*} $$ Every term is half of the previous and also half of what’s needed to reach $1$. Therefore, $(S_k) \to 1$, and so $$ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^n} = 1. $$ It can be a little counterintuitive that adding up infinitely many numbers, even if they’re getting smaller, can ever result in something finite. Conveniently, this sum in particular has a famous visualization that can make it much easier to digest. Begin with a square of side length $1$ and divide it into two equal rectangles of area $\frac{1}{2}$. Shade one and divide the other into two equal squares of area $\frac{1}{4}$. Shade one, divide the other, and repeat. As the number of divisions gets larger and larger, the unshaded area vanishes to zero, and the total area approaches $1$. !iframe https://www.desmos.com/calculator/cijszmjfj8 !end-example !begin-exercise convergence of series Evaluate the following series or show they don’t converge. 1. $\sum_{i = 0}^\infty (-1)^i$. 2. $\sum_{m = 1}^\infty \frac{2}{3^m}$. 3. $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n}$. Note: this is called the Harmonic series, and is quite famous. To get a handle on its behavior, try grouping the terms in this manner: put the first and second terms by themselves, then put the third and fourth together, then the fifth through the eighth together, then the 9th through the 16th, and so on, doubling the size of each group. !end-exercise !begin-text-block Since series are determined by their sequences of partial sums, many convenient properties of sequences carry over to series. !end-text-block !begin-prop properties of series Suppose $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n = a$ and $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty b_n = b$. Then: 1. $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty (a_n + b_n) = a + b$. 2. For any constant $c$, $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty ca_n = c\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n$. !end-prop !begin-text-block These results are pretty predictable: the first just says that infinite addition is commutative, and the second that it’s distributive with respect to multiplication. !end-text-block !section !text s Geometric Series
!begin-text-block In the last section, we introduced geometric sequences. These are sequences for which every term can be obtained from the previous one by multiplying by some factor $r$. In other words, they’re sequences of the form $$ (a, ar, ar^2, ar^3, ...) $$ for some values of $a$ and $r$. This pattern is particularly useful for series, and it’s worth devoting a fair bit of time to. !end-text-block !begin-def geometric series A geometric series is the sum of a geometric sequence — that is, a series of the form $$ \sum_{n = 0}^\infty ar^n = a\sum_{n = 0}^\infty r^n. $$ The first term of the series must be a constant term (i.e. $r^0$) in order for it to be geometric. !end-def !begin-text-block Let’s evaluate $\sum_{n = 0}^\infty r^n$. The $k$th partial sum is $$ S_k = 1 + r + r^2 + \cdots + r^{k - 1}. $$ While this may look pretty complicated, it’s actually a rather fragile expression. Watch what happens when we multiply $S_k$ by $r$: $$ rS_k = r + r^2 + r^3 + \cdots + r^k. $$ Every term there except for $r^k$ appears in the original expression of $S_k$ — they’re begging to be canceled out. $$ S_k - rS_k = (1 + r + r^2 + \cdots + r^{k - 1}) - (r + r^2 + r^3 + \cdots + r^k) = 1 - r^k. $$ Factoring and dividing, we have $$ \begin{align*} (1 - r)S_k &= 1 - r^k\\ S_k &= \frac{1 - r^k}{1 - r}. \end{align*} $$ It almost seems like we pulled a trick somewhere, but this is correct: no matter how large $k$ gets and how many terms are in $S_k$, we can always collapse it down to this style of fraction. Now the only question is, when does $(S_k)$ converge? We talked in the last section about the sequence $(r^k)$: it converges to zero when $-1 < r < 1$, to one when $r = 1$, and diverges otherwise. First of all, if $r = 1$, the original series is $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty 1$, which clearly diverges. If $(r^k)$ diverges, then so does $S_k$, since it contains a copy of $r^k$. But when $(r^k) \to 0$, then $(S_k) \to \frac{1}{1 - r}$. Therefore, we’re left with the following result: !end-text-block !begin-thm the values of geometric series Let $r$ be a constant with $-1 < r < 1$. Then $$ \sum_{n = 0}^\infty r^n = \frac{1}{1 - r}. $$ !end-thm !begin-example the value of a geometric series We can put this theorem to the test on the series from before: $\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^n}$. This doesn’t quite fit the definition of a geometric series, since the first power is $1$ and not $0$. However, we can just start the series at $n = 0$ and remove the first term afterward. $$ \begin{align*} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^n} &= \left( \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{1}{2^n} \right) - 1\\ &= \left( \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n \right) - 1\\ &= \frac{1}{1 - \frac{1}{2}} - 1\\ &= \frac{1}{\frac{1}{2}} - 1\\ &= 2 - 1\\ &= 1. \end{align*} $$ This agrees with the result from earlier! !end-example !begin-exercise the value of a geometric series Evaluate the following series. 1. $\sum_{k = 1}^\infty \frac{2^{k - 1}}{3^{k + 1}}$. 2. $\sum_{m = 1}^\infty e^m$. !end-exercise !section !text s Telescoping Series
!begin-text-block There’s one more kind of series to touch on before we wrap up this introductory section. If the process of adding up a series makes almost all of the terms cancel out, then we wind up with a very concise expression for the final value. !end-text-block !begin-def telescoping series A telescoping series is one for which almost all of the terms cancel with each other. !end-def !begin-text-block The simplest type of telescoping series is any series of the form $$ \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (a_{n + 1} - a_n). $$ Then the $k$th partial sum is just $$ S_k = (a_1 - a_0) + (a_2 - a_1) + \cdots + (a_k - a_{k - 1}) = a_k - a_0. $$ Therefore, the sequence of partial sums converging is equivalent to the sequence $(a_k)$ converging. If that is the case, and $(a_k) \to a$ for some $a$, then $$ \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (a_{n + 1} - a_n) = a - a_0. $$ !end-text-block !begin-example a telescoping series Evaluate the sum $$ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \left( \cos \left( \frac{1}{n} \right) - \cos \left( \frac{1}{n + 1} \right) \right). $$ This series telescopes: every $\cos \left( \frac{1}{n + 1} \right)$ cancels with the $\left( \cos \left( \frac{1}{n} \right)$ in the next term, making the partial sums have the form $$ S_k = \cos \left( \frac{1}{1} \right) - \cos \left( \frac{1}{k + 1} \right) $$ Moreover, as $k \to \infty$, $\frac{1}{k + 1} \to 0$, so $\left( \cos \left( \frac{1}{k + 1} \right) \to 1$. Therefore, our sum converges to $\cos(1) - 1$. !end-example !begin-exercise a telescoping series The series $$ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n(n + 1)} $$ is actually telescoping, even if it doesn’t look like it at first glance. Use partial fractions to split apart $\frac{1}{n(n + 1)}$, and then evaluate the sum using properties of telescoping series. !end-exercise !nav-buttons !footer
