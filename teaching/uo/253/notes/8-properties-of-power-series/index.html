!header Section 8: Properties of Power Series !nav-buttons !begin-text-block When series only contained numbers, we just had to understand them to the extent of determining whether they converged or not. Now that the terms have variables in them — in particular, different powers in each term, so that nothing can be combined — we need to be more comfortable dealing with series as objects in and of themselves, and not just unresolved sums that may or may not converge. !end-text-block !section !text s Adding Power Series
!begin-text-block The first and simplest thing we can do to power series is to add two of them together. Thankfully, this works as well as we could hope. !end-text-block !begin-prop adding power series Let $\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n x^n$ and $\sum_{n = 0}^\infty b_n x^n$ be two power series that converge to the functions $f$ and $g$ on the intervals $I$ and $J$, respectively. Then the power series $$ \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (a_n + b_n) x^n $$ converges to $f + g$ on the overlap of $I$ and $J$. !end-prop !begin-example adding power series Express the function $$ f(x) = \frac{5}{(1 - 2x)(1 + x^2)} $$ as a power series and determine the interval of convergence. While we can express this as two series multiplied together, that’s not ideal, and we don’t know how to handle that sort of construction yet. Instead, let’s turn to an old friend: partial fractions. That will let us write $f$ as the sum of two much simpler power series that we understand well, and then we can combine them using the previous proposition. Partial fractions tells us that for some numbers $A$ and $B$, $$ \frac{5}{(1 - 2x)(1 + x^2)} = \frac{A}{1 - 2x} + \frac{Bx + C}{1 + x^2}. $$ In other words, $$ 5 = A(1 + x^2) + (Bx + C)(1 - 2x) = (A + C) + (B - 2C)x + (A - 2B)x^2. $$ We then separate this into three equations by equating the coefficients. $$ \begin{align*} 5 &= A + C\\ 0 &= B - 2C\\ 0 &= A - 2B. \end{align*} $$ Working this out, we wind up with $$ \begin{align*} A &= 4\\ B &= 2\\ C &= 1. \end{align*} $$ Therefore, our expansion of the series $f$ becomes $$ f(x) = \frac{4}{1 - 2x} + \frac{2x}{1 + x^2} + \frac{1}{1 + x^2}. $$ Now all three of these terms have geometric series representations — we looked at many like them in the last section. First of all, $$ \frac{1}{1 - 2x} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (2x)^n = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (2^n x^n), $$ which converges when $|2x| < 1$, so $|x| < \frac{1}{2}$. Therefore, the interval of convergence is $\left( -\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2} \right)$. For the other denominator, we have $$ \frac{1}{1 + x^2} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-x^2)^n = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty ((-1)^n x^{2n}). $$ This series converges when $|-x^2| < 1$, so $|x| < 1$. The interval of convergence is therefore $(-1, 1)$. Let’s put it all together. The sum of all three terms becomes $$ \begin{align*} f(x) &= \frac{4}{1 - 2x} + \frac{x}{1 + x^2} + \frac{1}{1 + x^2}\\ &= 4\sum_{n = 0}^\infty (2^n x^n) + 2x\sum_{n = 0}^\infty ((-1)^n x^{2n}) + \sum_{n = 0}^\infty ((-1)^n x^{2n})\\ &= \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (4 \cdot 2^n x^n + 2 \cdot (-1)^n x^{2n + 1} + (-1)^n x^{2n}). \end{align*} $$ The two intervals of convergence are $\left( -\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2} \right)$ and $(-1, 1)$, and the overlap of those two is just the first one — therefore, the interval of convergence of our very complicated series is just $\left( -\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2} \right)$. !end-example !begin-exercise adding power series Express the function $$ g(x) = \frac{6}{(1 - x)(2 - x)(3 - x)} $$ as a power series and determine the interval of convergence. !end-exercise !begin-text-block For many series, we now have the tools to go backward: determine the function it’s converging to given just the series. !end-text-block !begin-example finding a closed form of a series Define a function $f$ by $$ f(x) = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty 2^{-2n}x^n. $$ Find a closed form for $f$ (i.e. a form without an infinite series). This looks a bit like a geometric series, but to apply the formula, we need every term to be raised to the power of $n$, not $-2n$. To that end, let’s rewrite $2^{-2n}$ as $4^{-n} = \left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^n$, so that the series becomes $$ \begin{align*} f(x) &= \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \left( \frac{1}{4} \right)^n x^n\\ &= \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \left( \frac{x}{4} \right)^n. \end{align*} $$ Now this is very close to a geometric series, but it’s starting at $n = 1$ instead of $n = 0$. To remedy that, we’ll start it at $0$ and then remove the first term later. $$ \begin{align*} f(x) &= -\left( \frac{x}{4} \right)^0 + \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left( \frac{x}{4} \right)^n\\ &= -1 + \frac{1}{1 - \frac{x}{4}}\\ &= -1 + \frac{1}{\frac{4 - x}{4}}\\ &= -1 + \frac{4}{4 - x}\\ &= \frac{x}{4 - x}. \end{align*} $$ !end-example !section !text s Multiplying Power Series
!begin-text-block The process of adding power series closely mirrors adding polynomials: we just add the coefficients of like terms. Multiplying polynomials is a much more complicated process, though, and multiplying power series will take some extra work to pin down. Let’s start with a motivating example and see what we can determine. Let $f(x) = 1 + 2x + 3x^2$ and $g(x) = 4 + 5x + 6x^2$. We normally write polynomials in descending order of their terms’ exponents, but this reverse order matches how power series are structured — low exponents come first. What is $f(x)g(x)$? We typically find this by foiling out the terms, but the end result is that every term of $f$ gets multiplied with every term of $g$, resulting in 9 terms total. Let’s arrange that in a table. $$ \begin{array}{r|ccc} & 4 & 5x & 6x^2\\ \hline 1 & 4 & 5x & 6x^2\\ 2x & 8x & 10x^2 & 12x^3\\ 3x^2 & 12x^2 & 15x^3 & 18x^4 \end{array} $$ Then the product $fg$ is just the sum of all nine entries in the table. There’s a cleaner way to think of it, though: have a look at the diagonals running from the bottom-left to the top-right. $$ \definecolor{textred}{rgb}{.85, 0, 0} \definecolor{textyellow}{rgb}{.85, .6, 0} \definecolor{textgreen}{rgb}{0, .6, 0} \definecolor{textcyan}{rgb}{0, .6, 1} \definecolor{textblue}{rgb}{0, 0, 1} \begin{array}{r|ccc} & 4 & 5x & 6x^2\\ \hline 1 & {\color{textred} 4} & {\color{textyellow} 5x} & {\color{textgreen} 6x^2}\\ 2x & {\color{textyellow} 8x} & {\color{textgreen} 10x^2} & {\color{textcyan} 12x^3}\\ 3x^2 & {\color{textgreen} 12x^2} & {\color{textcyan} 15x^3} & {\color{textblue} 18x^4} \end{array} $$ Here I’ve color-coded the table by diagonals. The key observation is that those diagonals correspond to different exponents on $x$: for example, the green diagonal in the middle contains every $x^2$ term. Therefore, if we want to know the coefficient on $x^2$ in $f(x)g(x)$, we just need to add up those three. Let’s make the jump to series. If $f(x) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n x^n$ and $g(x) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty b_n x^n$, then the product $f(x)g(x)$ will be the sum of an infinite table: $$ \begin{array}{r|ccccc} & b_0 & b_1 x & b_2 x^2 & b_3 x^3 & \cdots\\ \hline a_0 & a_0 b_0 & a_0 b_1 x & a_0 b_2 x^2 & a_0 b_3 x^3 & \cdots\\ a_1 x & a_1 b_0 x & a_1 b_1 x^2 & a_1 b_2 x^3 & a_1 b_3 x^4 & \cdots\\ a_2 x^2 & a_2 b_0 x^2 & a_2 b_1 x^3 & a_2 b_2 x^4 & a_2 b_3 x^5 & \cdots\\ a_3 x^3 & a_3 b_0 x^3 & a_3 b_1 x^4 & a_3 b_2 x^5 & a_3 b_3 x^6 & \cdots\\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{array} $$ To extract the coefficient of $x^n$ in $fg$, we need to sum the $n$th diagonal. Now all of the terms $a_n x^n$ and $b_n x^n$ have the convenient property that their coefficients’ subscripts match the exponent on $x$, and multiplying two terms together makes the exponents add together. Therefore, the terms in the $n$th diagonal of our infinite table are the products of terms whose subscripts add to $n$. For instance, the sum of the $n = 3$ diagonal is $$ (a_0 b_3 + a_1 b_2 + a_2 b_1 + a_3 b_0) x^3. $$ To express this symbolically, the subscript on $a$ runs from $0$ to $3$ while the subscript on $b$ runs from $3$ down to $0$. In general, this looks like $$ \left( \sum_{k = 0}^n a_k b_{n - k} \right) x^n. $$ In total, the product of our two power series becomes $$ f(x)g(x) = \left(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n x^n \right) \left(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty b_n x^n \right) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left( \sum_{k = 0}^n a_k b_{n - k} \right) x^n. $$ !end-text-block !begin-exercise multiplying power series earlier in the section, we expressed the function $$ f(x) = \frac{5}{(1 - 2x)(1 + x^2)} $$ as a power series using partial fractions. Instead, express it as the product of two power series and verify that it matches the previous result through the first six terms. !end-exercise !nav-buttons !footer
