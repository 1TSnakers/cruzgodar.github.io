!header Simple Applications !begin-text-block With the hardest conceptual work behind us, let’s turn to some simple applications of integrals. The first is one we’ve touched on before, and that you probably saw plenty of in calc I: the relationship between position, velocity, and acceleration. Recall that displacement means signed distance: if you move $3$ meters right and $2$ left, then your net displacement is only $1$ meter, while your total distance traveled is $5$. !end-text-block !begin-example displacement A particle is traveling in a straight line with a velocity of $v(t) = 3t - 5 \frac{m}{s}$ after $t$ seconds. Find the net displacement of the particle over $3$ seconds and its total distance traveled. Let’s start with net displacement, which is $s(3) - s(0)$. Since $s$ is an antiderivative of $v$, we have that $$ \begin{align*} s(3) - s(0) &= \int_0^3 v(t)\ dt\\ &= \int_0^3 (3t - 5)\ dt\\ &= \left. \left[ \frac{3}{2}t^2 - 5t \right] \right|_0^3\\ &= \left( \frac{3}{2} \cdot 9 - 15 \right) - 0\\ &= -\frac{3}{2}. \end{align*} $$ Therefore, the particle ended up $1.5$ meters to the left of where it started after $3$ seconds. This is just the end result, though, and it doesn't tell us how much the particle moved in total. For that, there’s an easy change we can make: rather than integrating $v$, which is sometimes negative and sometimes positive, we’ll integrate $|v|$. This makes all velocity count as positive, whether it’s to the left or right, and so integrating that will give us total distance traveled. There’s some nuance to just taking that absolute value, though, because we need to know when $v$ is positive and when it’s negative. To do this, we’ll first set it equal to zero: if $3t - 5 = 0$, then $t = \frac{5}{3}$, and since $v$ is a line with positive slope, it must be negative for $t < \frac{5}{3}$ and positive for $t > \frac{5}{3}$. In order to take the absolute value, we need to negate $v$ when it’s negative and not when it’s positive. This means doing different things on different parts of the domain, so we need to split up the integral itself. If we let $s_{\text{tot}}(t)$ be the total distance traveled after $t$ seconds, then we have $$ \begin{align*} s_{\text{tot}}(3) - s_{\text{tot}}(0) &= \int_0^3 |v(t)|\ dt\\ &= \int_0^{\frac{5}{3}} -v(t)\ dt + \int_{\frac{5}{3}}^3 v(t)\ dt\\ &= -\int_0^{\frac{5}{3}} (3t - 5)\ dt + \int_{\frac{5}{3}}^3 (3t - 5)\ dt\\ &= -\left. \left[ \frac{3}{2}t^2 - 5t \right] \right|_0^{\frac{5}{3}} + \left. \left[ \frac{3}{2}t^2 - 5t \right] \right|_{\frac{5}{3}}^3\\ &= -\left( \frac{3}{2} \cdot \frac{25}{9} - \frac{25}{3} \right) - 0 + \left( \frac{3}{2} \cdot 9 - 15 \right) - \left( \frac{3}{2} \cdot \frac{25}{9} - \frac{25}{3} \right)\\ &= \frac{41}{6}. \end{align*} $$ The particle therefore traveled a total of $\frac{41}{6} \approx 6.83$ meters total.
!end-example !begin-exercise displacement A particle moves at velocity $v(t) = 4t^2 - 16t + 12$. Find its net displacement and total distance traveled between time $t = 0$ and $t = 4$. !end-exercise !begin-text-block Let’s finish the section with something a little easier. You might have seen even and odd functions before, but we’ll briefly review what they are. !end-text-block !begin-def even and odd functions A function $f$ is even if $f(-x) = f(x)$ for all $x$ in its domain, and odd if $f(-x) = -f(x)$ for all $x$ in its domain. !end-def !begin-text-block Geometrically, even functions are ones whose graphs are symmetric about the $y$-axis and odd functions are ones whose graphs are symmetric when rotated 180 degrees about the origin. For example, $x^4 - x^2$ is even, while $x^3$ is odd. !end-text-block !iframe https://www.desmos.com/calculator/xch7oxgfa4 !begin-prop integrals of even and odd functions Let $f$ be an even function. Then $\int_{-a}^a f(x)\ dx = 2\int_0^a f(x)\ dx.$ Let $g$ be an odd function. Then $\int_{-a}^a f(x)\ dx = 0.$ !end-prop !begin-text-block This shouldn’t be terribly surprising. If a function is even and we’re integrating over an interval centered at $0$, then the total area is evenly split over the parts to the left and right of $x = 0$. If the function is odd, then those two areas exactly cancel out instead. !end-text-block !begin-example integrals of even and odd functions We have $$ \begin{align*} \int_{-2}^2 (x^4 - x^2)\ dx &= 2\int_0^3 (x^4 - x^2)\ dx\\ &= 2 \left. \left[ \frac{x^5}{5} - \frac{x^3}{3} \right] \right|_0^2\\ &= 2 \left(\frac{32}{5} - \frac{8}{3}\right)\\ &= \frac{112}{15}. \end{align*} $$ And $$ \int_{-2}^2 (x^5)\ dx = 0. $$ !end-example !begin-exercise integrals of even and odd functions Find $\int_{-\pi}^\pi \sin(\theta)\ d\theta$ and $\int_{-\pi}^\pi \cos(\theta)\ d\theta$ by using properties of even and odd functions. !end-exercise !begin-text-block In the next section, we’ll start talking about the first technique we have to calculate more complicated integrals. !end-text-block !begin-text-buttons previous l Previous home l Home next l Next !end-text-buttons !footer
