!header Section 7: Exponential, Logarithmic, and Inverse Trig Integrals !begin-text-block So far, we know the antiderivatives of functions which are derivatives of other functions, and we can also sometimes compose them with $u$-sub. In this section, we’ll talk about a few more common integrals, and then we’ll end with a summary of all the integrals we know so far. !end-text-block !begin-example An integral involving an exponential function A certain strain of bacteria grow at a rate of $3^t$ new bacteria per hour. If the population starts at $100$ bacteria at time $0$, how many will there be after $6$ hours? Let $N(t)$ be the number of total bacteria after $t$ hours. Then $N(0) = 0$ and $N'(t) = 3^t$, since the $3^t$ is the rate of growth of $N$. We want to find $N(6)$, so a good place to start is $N(t)$ in general. This is an antiderivative of $N'$, and so we have $$ \begin{align*} N(t) &= \int N'(t)\ dt\\ &= \int 3^t\ dt. \end{align*} $$ Since $\frac{d}{dt} [3^t] = 3^t \ln(3)$, we can divide both sides by $\ln(3)$ to find that $N(t) = \int 3^t\ dt = \frac{1}{ln(3)} 3^t + C$. Since $N(0) = 100$, we have that $100 = \frac{1}{ln(3)} 3^0 + C$, so $C = 100 - \frac{1}{\ln(3)} \approx 99.09$. Therefore, $N(6) = \frac{3^6}{\ln(3)} + 99.09 = 762.7$. !end-example !begin-exercise An integral involving an exponential function A radioactive substance degrades at a rate of $-.0953 \cdot 1.1^{-t}$ grams per year after $t$ years. If there is initially $1$ gram of the substance, how much will be left after $20$ years? !end-exercise !begin-example Another exponential integral Find $\int \frac{2^{1/x}}{x^2}\ dx. This is too complicated to evaluate directly, so we have to try $u$-sub. If we set $u = x^2$, then the exponential becomes $2^{1/\sqrt{u}}$, which is not helpful. Instead, we can try $u = \frac{1}{x}$. Then $du = -\frac{1}{x^2}\ dx$. This lines up nicely: the integral becomes $$ \begin{align*} -\int 2^u\ du &= -\frac{1}{\ln(2)} 2^u + C\\ &= -\frac{1}{\ln(2)} 2^(1/x) + C. \end{align*} $$ !end-example !begin-text-block We know that $\int \frac{1}{x}\ dx = \ln|x| + C$, since $\frac{d}{dx}[\ln|x|] = \frac{1}{x}$, but we don’t yet know the integral of $\ln(x)$, or more generally of $\log_a(x)$ for any $a$. Let’s fix that now. !end-text-block !begin-prop the integral of natural log $\int \ln(x)\ dx = x\ln(x) - x + C$. !end-prop !begin-exercise Differentiate $x\ln(x) - x + C$ and verify that you do in fact get $\ln(x)$. !end-exercise !begin-text-block It’s not at all clear how we were supposed to find this integral out, but it isn’t all that hard to verify. Now we can integrate functions involving $\ln(x)$, and for logs with different bases, we can use the following formula: $$ \log_a(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(a)}. $$ Remember that $\log_a(x)$ is the number you raise $a$ to in order to get $x$ — for example, $\log_2(8) = 3$, since $2^3 = 8$. And $\ln(x)$ is just a shorthand for $\log_e(x)$. !end-text-block !begin-example a logarithmic integral Find $\int_2^3 \log_2(5x)\ dx$.
