!header Section 8: The Area Between Curves !nav-buttons !begin-text-block Up to this point, we’ve defined integrals via Riemann sums, related them to antiderivatives via the Fundamental Theorem, and then made taking antiderivatives a little easier with $u$-sub. Now, we’ll extend what definite integrals can do by exploring how they can calculate area — not just under a curve, but between two of them. !end-text-block !begin-prop area between curves Let $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ be functions with $f(x) \geq g(x)$ on $[a, b]$. Then the area between the graphs of $f$ and $g$ on $[a, b]$ is $$ \int_a^b (f(x) - g(x))\,dx. $$ !end-prop !begin-example area between curves Find the area of the region bounded by $f(x) = x$ and $g(x) = x^2$. First, we need to actually figure out where that area is, so that we know the limits of integration. !desmos area-between-curves To find those limits, we set the two functions equal and solve for $x$. If $x = x^2$, then $x^2 - x = 0$, so $x(x-1) = 0$, and therefore $x = 0$ or $x = 1$. This reflects what we see in the graph. Since $f(x)$ is the function with the larger $y$-value on the region, our integral becomes $$ \begin{align*} \int_0^1 (x - x^2)\,dx &= \left. \left[ \frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{x^3}{3} \right] \right|_0^1\\ &= \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3}\\ &= \frac{1}{6}. \end{align*} $$ !end-example !begin-exercise area between curves Find the area of the region bounded by $y = 6 - x$ and $y = 9 - \left( \frac{x}{2} \right)^2$. !end-exercise !begin-example more area between curves Find the area between $\sin(x)$ and $\cos(x)$ on $[0, \pi]$. This is a little more complicated: which function is on top changes across the interval. Setting $\sin(x) = \cos(x)$ is a problem from trigonometry: dividing both sides by $\cos(x)$ results in $\tan(x) = 1$, which on our interval is only true at $x = \frac{\pi}{4}$. So the functions meet at $x = \frac{\pi}{4}$, and since they’re both continuous, that’s the only possible place they could cross. Let’s try some test points: plugging in $x = 0$ gives $\sin(0) = 0$ and $\cos(0) = 1$, so until $x = \frac{\pi}{4}$, $\cos$ is the larger function. Similarly, $x = \pi$ gives $\sin(\pi) = 0$ and $\cos(\pi) = -1$, so $\sin$ is larger after $\frac{\pi}{4}$. Therefore, we have $$ \begin{align*} &\ \ \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{4}} (\cos(x) - \sin(x))\,dx + \int_{\frac{\pi}{4}}^\pi (\sin(x) - \cos(x))\,dx\\ &= \left. \left[ \sin(x) + \cos(x) \right] \right|_0^{\frac{\pi}{4}} + \left. \left[ -\cos(x) - \sin(x) \right] \right|_{\frac{\pi}{4}}^\pi\\ &= \left( \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} - 1 - 0 \right) + \left( 1 + 0 + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \right)\\ &= 2\sqrt{2}. \end{align*} $$ !desmos area-between-curves-2 !end-example !begin-example even more area between curves Find the area of the shaded region bounded by $y = 1$, $y = 3 - x$, and $y = x^2 + 1$, as pictured. !desmos area-between-curves-3 First, let’s find the limits of integration. The left one is the intersection of $y = x^2 + 1$ with $y = 1$: then $x^2 + 1 = 1$, so $x = 0$. The right endpoint is the intersection of $y = 3 - x$ with $y = 1$, so $x = 2$. Like the last example, though, the bounding functions change as we move left to right along the interval. The point where we switch from $x^2 + 1$ to $3 - x$ being the top function is the intersection of the two: $x^2 + 1 = 3 - x$, so $x^2 + x - 2 = 0$. Factoring this gives $x = 1$ or $x = -2$, and we clearly want $x = 1$. Now the integral becomes $$ \begin{align*} \int_0^1 (x^2 + 1 - 1)\,dx + \int_1^2 (3 - x - 1)\,dx &= \left. \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} \right] \right|_0^1 + \left. \left[ 2x - \frac{x^2}{2} \right] \right|_1^2\\ &= \frac{1}{3} + \left( 4 - 2 - 2 + \frac{1}{2} \right)\\ &= \frac{5}{6}. \end{align*} $$ !end-example !begin-exercise even more area between curves Find the area of the shaded region bounded by $y = 1 - x$, $y = x^2$, and $y = \sqrt{x}$, as pictured. !desmos area-between-curves-4 !end-exercise !begin-text-block Sometimes — particularly in cases like the previous example with multiple bounding functions, we can make things much simpler by changing the variable we integrate with respect to. This is not $u$-sub, but something else entirely: integrating with respect to $y$. Let’s redo the previous example to see how it works. !end-text-block !begin-example integrating with respect to $y$. Find the area of the shaded region bounded by $y = 1$, $y = 3 - x$, and $y = x^2 + 1$ by integrating with respect to $y$. !desmos area-between-curves-5 The first thing to do is rewrite the functions as functions of $y$. That means solving each one for $x$: $y = 3 - x$ becomes $x = 3 - y$, and $y = x^2 + 1$ becomes $x = \sqrt{y - 1}$. Since there is no $x$ in $y = 1$, we can’t solve it for $x$, but we’ll see that that won’t be a problem momentarily. Now we need to identify the limits, which are $y$-values now. The minimum $y$-value is clearly $y = 1$, and to find the maximum one, we intersect the other two functions. That gave us $x = 1$ before, but we want the $y$-value this time, so we can just plug $x = 1$ into either formula to get $y = 2$. Alternatively, we could have just solved $\sqrt{y - 1} = 3 - y$. The next thing to reverse is the way the integral is set up. Normally we take the top function minus the bottom one — i.e. the larger $y$-value minus the smaller $y$-value. Now we’re using $x$ instead, so to take the larger $x$-value minus the smaller $x$-value, we take the right function minus the left one. And here’s the reason this is an easier way to do things for this example in particular: the right and left functions stay the same across the entire region, so there’s no need to split up the region. We just have $$ \begin{align*} \int_1^2 (3 - y - \sqrt{y - 1})\ dy &= \int_1^2 (3 - y)\ dy - \int_1^2 \sqrt{y - 1}\ dy\\ & u = y - 1,\ du = dy\\ &= \int_1^2 (3 - y)\ dy - \int_1^2 \sqrt{u}\ du\\ &= \left. \left[ 3y - \frac{y^2}{2} \right] \right|_1^2 - \left. \left[ \frac{u^{3/2}}{3/2} \right] \right|_1^2\\ &= \left. \left[ 3y - \frac{y^2}{2} \right] \right|_1^2 - \left. \left[ \frac{(y-1)^{3/2}}{3/2} \right] \right|_1^2\\ &= \left( 6 - 2 - 3 + \frac{1}{2} \right) - \left( \frac{1}{3/2} \right)\\ &= \frac{3}{2} - \frac{2}{3}\\ &= \frac{5}{6}, \end{align*} $$ which agrees with what we got before. !end-example !begin-exercise even more area between curves Find the area of the shaded region bounded by $y = 1 - x$, $y = x^2$, and $y = \sqrt{x}$ by integrating with respect to $y$. !desmos area-between-curves-6 !end-exercise !begin-text-block In general, you’ll need to choose which variable is better to integrate with respect to. Sometimes one will be a little easier than the other, and other times only one will be possible at all. Integrating with respect to $y$ will continue to crop up throughout the coming sections, so it’s a good thing to get accustomed to sooner rather than later. !end-text-block !nav-buttons !footer
