!header Section 11: Physical Applications !begin-text-block In the final (thankfully!) section of applications of integration, we’ll talk about a few miscellaneous topics that arean’t large enough to get their own sections: work, mass, and tanks. Let’s start with the simplest part: work. If you’ve taken a physics course, you might know that work is loosely defined to be force times distance. Force is measured in Newtons ($N$), so for example, if you apply a $3\ N$ force to a cart in order to move it $9\ m$, then you do a total of $3 \cdot 9 = 27\ Nm$ of work. Like the previous problems we’ve looked at, we’ll be discussing problems in which the force you apply changes over time, and just like with everything else we’ve done with integrals so far, we’ll handle this by slicing the distance covered into tiny pieces, and integrating them. !end-text-block !begin-thm work done by moving an object Suppose an object is moved from $x = a$ to $x = b$, and the force done at position $x$ is $F(x)$. Then the total work done is $$ \int_a^b F(x)\ dx. $$ !end-thm !begin-example work done by moving an object A cart is pushed $5$ meters by applying a force of $x^2$ Newtons when the cart has been pushed $x$ meters. Find the total work done. This is just $\int_0^5 x^2\ dx = \left. \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} \right] \right|_0^5 = \frac{125}{3}\ Nm$. !end-example !begin-exercise work done by stopping an object A cart is rolling down a hill, and you try to stop it. You apply a force of $x\ N$ against the cart when it has rolled $x\ m$, and it finally comes to a stop after $3$ meters. How much work do you do? Note that force applied against the direction of motion counts as negative. !end-exercise !begin-text-block Moving on to other work problems, let’s look at springs. We can do this using something called Hooke’s law, which tells us how much force is required to stretch or compress a spring in terms of its strength. !end-text-block !begin-thm Hooke’s law Every spring has an associated constant $k$, called the spring constant. When the spring has been stretched $x$ meters, the force to stretch it more is $kx$. Similarly, when it has been compressed $x$ meters, the force to compress it more is $kx$. !end-thm !begin-example Hooke’s law It takes $10\ Nm$ of work to compress a spring $.2\ m$ from its resting position. How much work is done if you stretch it $.5\ m$ from its resting position? The work done to compress the spring is $\int_0^{.2} kx\ dx = \frac{.2^2}{2}k = .02k$. Now this is equal to $10$, so we have that $k = 500$. Now in order to stretch the spring $.5\ m$, we have $\int_0^{.5} 500x\ dx = 500 \cdot \frac{.5^2}{2} = 62.5\ Nm$. !end-example !begin-exercise Hooke’s law A spring is stretched $1\ m$, and it takes a force of $5\ N$ to stretch it more at that point. How much work is done by compressing the spring $.1\ m$ from its resting position? !end-exercise !section !text s Mass and Density
