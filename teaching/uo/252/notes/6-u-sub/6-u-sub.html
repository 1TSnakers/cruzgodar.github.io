!header Section 6: $u$-Substitution !text b c Covered Wednesday of Week 3
!begin-text-block At the moment, the only reasonable way we can calculate integrals is with the fundamental theorem, which requires taking an antiderivative. With derivatives, we know a handful of them exactly, like $\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(x)] = \cos(x)$, and we developed a handful of rules like the product and chain rules to be able to differentiate more complicated functions. We already have that first part done for antiderivatives, since we can just run all the regular derivatives backward: for example, $\int \cos(x)\ dx = \sin(x) + C$. What we need, though, is rules analogous to the ones for derivatives, only in reverse. We’ll start with the simplest and arguably most useful: the opposite of the chain rule. !end-text-block !begin-thm $u$-substitution If you have an integral of the form $$ \int f(g(x))g'(x)\ dx, $$ then: 1. Set $u = g(x)$. 2. Write $\frac{du}{dx} = g'(x)$ and multiply both sides by $dx$ to get $du = g'(x) dx$. (This looks weird, but it’s okay.) 3. Rewrite the integral as $\int f(u)\ du$. 4. Integrate as usual to get $F(u) + C$. 5. Back-substitute for $u$ to get $F(g(x)) + C$. !end-thm !begin-text-block This really is just the chain rule backward: since $F' = f$, we have $\frac{d}{dx}[F(g(x))] = f(g(x))g'(x)$. In practice, $u$-sub lets us integrate a composition, as long as the derivative of the inside function is being multiplied on the outside one. !end-text-block !begin-example $u$-sub Find $\int 6x(3x^2 + 4)^4\ dx$. We could foil out $(3x^2 + 4)^4$, but that’s way more work than necessary. Instead, we can think of it as a composition of two functions, where the outside function is $x^4$ and the inside one is $3x^2 + 4$. Since $\frac{d}{dx} \left[ 3x^2 + 4 \right] = 6x$, we set $u = 3x^2 + 4$ and $du = 6x\ dx$. Then we have $$ \begin{align*} \int 6x(3x^2 + 4)^4\ dx &= \int u^4\ du\\ &= \frac{u^5}{5}\\ &= \frac{(3x^2 + 4)^5}{5}. \end{align*} $$ !end-example !begin-exercise $u$-sub Find $\int 2\sin^3(2x)\cos(2x)\ dx$. Solution: Let $u = \sin(2x)$. (Another possible value is $u = 2x$, but that won’t advance the problem forward — if you don’t see why, try it!) Then $du = 2\cos(2x)\ dx$ by the chain rule, and so the integral becomes $$ \begin{align*} \int u^3\ du &= \frac{u^4}{4} + C\\ &= \frac{1}{4} \sin^4(2x) + C. \end{align*} $$ !end-exercise !begin-text-block The chain rule works for all compositions, but $u$-sub works only in the specific situations where the derivative of the inside is present on the outside. Unfortunately, if this isn’t the case, there’s usually not a way to get around it. !end-text-block !begin-example $u$-sub doesn’t always work Find $\int 6x^2(3x^2 + 4)^4\ dx$. If we try to set $u = 3x^2 + 4$ again, we get that $du = 6x\ dx$, as before. The integral then becomes $\int xu^4\ du$, and as is, that’s a deadend, because the integral contains more than just $u$. The only way to possibly make it work is to solve for $x$ in terms of $u$, which gives $x = \sqrt{\frac{1}{3} (u - 4)}$. But the integral is then $\int u^4\sqrt{\frac{1}{3} (u - 4)}\ du$, and this is significantly worse than what we started with. Unfortunately, there is simply no way to make $u$-sub helpful on this problem. Eventually, we’ll talk about a different strategy to handle this kind of integral, but that’s for the future. !end-example !begin-example $u$-sub Find $\int_3^4 z\sqrt{z^2 - 5}\ dz$. When using $u$-sub on a definite integral, we need to be careful. The $3$ and $4$ are things to plug in for $z$, not $u$, so we’ll need to remember to backsubstitute before plugging in the limits. $$ \begin{align*} \int_3^4 z\sqrt{z^2 - 5}\ dz\\ u &= z^2 - 5\\ du &= 2z\ dz \end{align*} $$ At this point, $du$ isn’t exactly present in the integral: it’s $2z\ dz$ and we only have $z\ dz$. However, since we’re only off by a constant multiple, we can just divide by it on both sides to make the expression fit what we have. $$ \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} du &= z\ dz\\ \int_3^4 z\sqrt{z^2 - 5}\ dz &= \int_3^4 \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{u}\ du\\ &= \int_3^4 \frac{1}{2} u^{1/2}\ du\\ &= \left. \left[ \frac{1}{2} \frac{u^{3/2}}{3/2} \right] \right|_3^4\\ &= \left. \left[ \frac{1}{3} u^{3/2} \right] \right|_3^4\\ \end{align*} $$ Now we need to backsubstitute before plugging in the limits. $$ \begin{align*} \left. \left[ \frac{1}{3} u^{3/2} \right] \right|_3^4 &= \left. \left[ \frac{1}{3} (z^2 - 5)^{3/2} \right] \right|_3^4\\ &= \frac{1}{3} 11^{3/2} - \frac{1}{3} 4^{3/2}. \end{align*} $$ !end-example !begin-exercise $u$-sub Find $\int 2t^2 e^{4t^3 + 3}\ dt$. Solution: let $u = 4t^3 + 3$. Then $du = 12t^2\ dt$, but we have $2t^2\ dt$, so we solve for $2t^2\ dt$ to get $2t^2\ dt = 6\ du$. Now we have $$ \begin{align*} \int 2t^2 e^{4t^3 + 3}\ dt &= \int 6 e^u\ du\\ &= 6e^u + C\\ &= 6e^{4t^3 + 3} + C. \end{align*} $$ !end-exercise !begin-text-block One common type of integral involves $\sin^2(x)$ or $\cos^2(x)$ (remember that those are just more convenient ways of writing $(\sin(x))^2$ and $(\cos(x))^2$). For these, we use a modified version of the half-angle formulas: !end-text-block !begin-prop the half-angle formulas $$ \sin^2(\theta) = \frac{1 - \cos(2\theta)}{2} $$ $$ \cos^2(\theta) = \frac{1 + \cos(2\theta)}{2} $$ !end-prop !begin-text-block Together, these let us integrate expressions involving $\cos^2(x)$ and $\sin^2(x)$. !end-text-block !begin-example the half-angle formulas Find $\int 3\cos^2(x)\ dx$. First, we need to apply the half-angle formula: $$ \begin{align*} \int 3\cos^2(x)\ dx &= 3\int \cos^2(x)\ dx\\ &= 3\int \frac{1 + \cos(2x)}{2}\ dx\\ &= 3\int \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\cos(2x)\ dx\\ &= \frac{3}{2}x + C + 3\int \frac{1}{2}\cos(2x)\ dx \end{align*} $$ To integrate $\cos(2x)$, we set $u = 2x$ and $du = 2\ dx$, so $dx = \frac{1}{2}\ du$. Now $$ \begin{align*} \frac{3}{2}x + C + 3\int \frac{1}{2}\cos(2x)\ dx &= \frac{3}{2}x + C + \frac{3}{4}\int \cos(u)\ du\\ &= \frac{3}{2}x + C + \frac{3}{4}\sin(u)\\ &= \frac{3}{2}x + \frac{3}{4}\sin(2x) + C. \end{align*} $$ Although we have two integrals, we only need one $C$, since it can take on any numerical value. !end-example !begin-exercise the half-angle formulas Find $\int \sin^4(t)\ dt$. Solution: using the half-angle formula, we have $$ \begin{align*} \int \sin^4(t)\ dt &= \int (\sin^2(t))^2\ dt\\ &= \int \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2} \cos(2t) \right)^2\ dt\\ &= \int (\frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{2} \cos(2t) + \frac{1}{4} \cos^2(2t))\ dt\\ &= \frac{1}{4} t - \frac{1}{4} \sin(2t) + \frac{1}{4} \int \cos^2(2t)\ dt\\ \end{align*} $$ Now we use half-angles again, this time on $\cos^2(2t)$. $$ \begin{align*} \int \cos^2(2t)\ dt &= \int \left( \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} \cos(4t) \right)\ dt\\ &= \frac{1}{2} t + \frac{1}{8} \cos(4t) + C. \end{align*} $$ In total, we have $$ \int \sin^4(t)\ dt = \frac{1}{4} t - \frac{1}{4} \sin(2t) + \frac{1}{4} \left(\frac{1}{2} t + \frac{1}{8} \cos(4t)\right) + C. $$ !end-exercise !begin-text-block One last type of $u$-sub problem to explore is where the substitution doesn’t quite work. We encountered this in an earlier example where $u$-sub didn’t work at all, but sometimes it can be made to work. !end-text-block !begin-example another $u$-sub Find $\int \frac{x}{\sqrt{x - 1}}\ dx$. The complicated part is the $\sqrt{x - 1}$, so let’s try setting $u$ to be the inside function. If $u = x - 1$, then $du = dx$, and so we have $$\int \frac{x}{\sqrt{x - 1}}\ dx = \int \frac{x}{\sqrt{u}}\ du.$$ To get rid of the $x$ left over, we need to solve for $x$ in terms of $u$: $x = u + 1$. Now we have $$ \begin{align*} \int \frac{u + 1}{\sqrt{u}}\ du &= \int \frac{u}{\sqrt{u}} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{u}}\ du\\ &= \int \frac{u}{\sqrt{u}}\ du + \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{u}}\ du\\ &= \int u^{1/2}\ du + \int u^{-1/2}\ du\\ &= \frac{2}{3} u^{3/2} + 2u^{1/2} + C\\ &= \frac{2}{3} (x - 1)^{3/2} + 2(x - 1)^{1/2} + C. \end{align*} $$ !end-example !nav-buttons !footer
