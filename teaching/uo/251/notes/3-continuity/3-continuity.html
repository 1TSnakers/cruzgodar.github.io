!begin-text-block We’ve informally defined $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ as being the value we would expect $f(a)$ to be, regardless of what $f(a)$ actually is, or even if it’s defined at all. When the value we expect to be there is actually what’s there, we have a special term for the function. !end-text-block !begin-def continuity Let $f(x)$ be a function. If $f(a)$ is defined and $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$, then we say that $f$ is continuous at $a$. If $f$ is continuous at all $a$ in its domain, then we say that $f$ is continuous. !end-def !nav-buttons !footer
