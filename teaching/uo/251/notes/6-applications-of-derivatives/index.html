!header Section 6: Applications of Derivatives !nav-buttons !begin-text-block While we’ve touched on a few applications of derivatives so far, let’s take a section to talk about a handful more. This won’t take us an incredibly long time, but there are some important points we haven’t touched on yet. The most important thing to mention is an alternate way to interpret derivatives. For some functions, we understand their rate of change as its own concept — the derivative of position is velocity, and we don’t need any metaphors to understand what a velocity function measures — it’s just speed in a certain direction. On the other hand, if we have a function $P(t)$ that gives population of a city after $t$ years of measurement, what exactly is $P'(t)$ doing? We’ve mentioned in class that it has units of people per year, but unlike velocity, which we understand in its own right, we don’t have a preconceived notion of what the instantaneous rate of change of population means. Thankfully, there is a sense in which we can understand it a little bit more easily. The derivative $f'(a)$ is the slope of a tangent line, which is just rise over run. When run is exactly $1$, the slope is just the rise. Since the tangent line by definition touches the graph at $(a, f(a))$, the tangent line can’t get that far away from the graph when $x$ increases by just $1$. In total, the derivative $f'(a)$ is approximately the difference between $f(a)$ and $f(a + 1)$. !iframe https://www.desmos.com/calculator/yga8xu0vp3 Here, the two green points indicate show where the tangent line predicts the point on the graph will be, and also where it actually is. When $a = 0$, $g'(a) = -\frac{1}{2}$, so $g(1) - g(0) \approx -\frac{1}{2}$. In reality, it’s actually $-\frac{3}{8}$, but that’s not too far off. The point is this: $g'(x) \approx g(x + 1) - g(x)$. For our population example, $P'(t)$ is approximately the amount the population increased between years $t$ and $t + 1$. We don’t just need to make the run exactly $1$, either. If it is $a$, then we get the following approximation: $$ a \cdot g'(x) \approx g(x + a) - g(x). $$ Therefore, $g(x + a) \approx g(x) + a \cdot g(x)$. For example, if the population in our town is $1000$ in the year $2010$, then $P(2010) = 1000$. Then if $P'(2010) = 500$, the population will increase by approximately $500$ by $2011$. Similarly, we expect that the population was about $750$ half a year after $2009$, $500$ in $2009$, and so on. !end-text-block !section !text s Average Velocity
!begin-text-block We’ve already talked about how derivatives relate to position, velocity, and acceleration: if those functions are given by $s(t)$, $v(t)$, and $a(t)$ respectively, then $v(t) = s'(t)$ and $a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)$. One more thing to mention is average velocity, which is defined to just be the change in position between two points in time, divided by the change in time. !end-text-block !begin-def average velocity Let $s(t)$ be the position of an object at time $t$. The average velocity between times $t = a$ and $t = b$ is $$ v_{avg} = \frac{s(b) - s(a)}{b - a}. $$ !end-def !begin-text-block This is how you probably approximate velocity in your head: if a car drives 45 miles in half in hour, it’s probably going about 90 miles an hour. The way we make that formal is with average velocity: over those 30 minutes, the car’s average velocity is 90 miles per hour. !end-text-block !section !text s Tangent Line Equations
!begin-text-block Another small topic to cover: the derivative of a function gives the slope of a tangent line, but what about the equation of that line? For that, we can use point-slope form: the equation of a line with slope $m$ passing through the point $(x_0, y_0)$ is $y = y_0 + m(x - x_0)$. When $x = a$, the tangent line to the graph of $f(x)$ passes through $(a, f(a))$ and has slope $f'(a)$, which means: !end-text-block !begin-prop tangent line equation Let $f$ be a function differentiable at $x = a$. Then the equation of the tangent line to $f$ at $x = a$ is $$ y = f(a) + f'(a) \cdot (x - a). $$ !end-prop !section !text s Applications to Business
!begin-text-block One last handful of applications: we can apply derivatives to some functions used in business and economics. We need a few quick definitions first. If we are selling items for profit, the cost function $C(x)$ gives the total cost to us for aquiring $x$ items — not the selling price, but the cost we pay to get the supply. Similarly, $R(x)$ is the revenue we receive from selling $x$ items. Finally, $P(x)$ is the profit we make from selling $x$ items — so $P(x) = R(x) - C(x)$. Of particular use to us are the derivatives of these functions. In the terminology of business applications, we call these marginal functions. The marginal cost function is $MC(x) = C'(x)$, and similarly for the marginal revenue and profit functions, $MR(x)$ and $MP(x)$. Using the interpretation of the derivative from earlier in the section, these marginal functions are approximately the cost, revenue, and profit we get from selling item number $x + 1$. For example, $MC(4) = C'(4) \approx C(5) - C(4)$, which is the cost to us to acquire the fifth item. !end-text-block !begin-example business applications You’re selling whiteboard markers. When you sell $x$ of them, you charge $p(x) = 1 - .01x$ dollars per marker. Each one costs you $5$ cents to produce. What is the profit you make from selling the 20th marker? The total cost to us to get $x$ markers is $\$0.05$ per marker times $x$ markers, so $C(x) = .05x$. Similarly, $R(x) = x \cdot p(x) = x - .01x^2$. Then $P(x) = R(x) - C(x) = .95x - .01x^2$. We need to compute $MP(x) = P'(x) = .95 - .02x$. Then the profit from item 20 is $MP(19) = .95 - .38 = .57$. Therefore, we make a profit of $57$ cents from that particular marker. !end-example !nav-buttons !footer
