!begin-text-block In section 1, we introduced limits, but the way we calculated them was often tedious or difficult. In sections 2 and 3, we introduced limit rules and continuous functions, both of which gave us tools to more easily and quickly calculate limits. After introducing derivatives in the previous section, it’s time to improve on the initial way we calculate them (called the limit definition of the derivative. We’ll build up a series of rules and techniques that let us differentiate many functions that were much too difficult with the limit definition. The derivative $f'(a)$ is just the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at $x = a$. If the graph of $f$ is already a horizontal line, then a tangent line at any point is just that same line, and therefore has slope zero. !end-text-block !iframe https://www.desmos.com/calculator/fwu2uv6gxj !begin-text-block The functions whose graphs are horizontal lines are the constant functions $y = c$ for some number $c$. Since the exact value of $c$ is irrelevant, we have the result that all constant functions have derivative zero. !end-text-block !begin-thm constant functions have derivative zero Let $f(x) = c$ for a constant $c$. Then $f'(x) = 0$. !end-thm !begin-text-block This is a good start! Just like we did with limits, let’s set our sights on polynomials next. There’s a couple things we’ll have to tackle to get there: for example, $2x^2 - x$ features the terms $x^2$ and $x$, along with subtraction and multiplication by $2$. With limits, we were able to ignore almost all of that, passing them through arithmetic, powers, and roots, but derivatives won’t be quite so fluid. Let’s begin with terms like $x$ and $x^2$. !end-text-block !begin-thm the derivative of $x^n$ Let $f(x) = x^n$, where $n$ is a positive integer. Then $f'(x) = nx^{n-1}$. You’ll often hear this referred to as the power rule, but in comparison to the techniques we’ll see later in this section, which let us differentiate combinations of any two functions, this is a much more narrowly-focused theorem. For that reason, along with some common mistakes we’ll mention later, I tend not to use that name. !end-thm !begin-text-block In other words, to differentiate a function of the form $x^n$, bring down the power in the form of a constant multiple and decrease the power by one. The proof of this theorem is a little bit hairy — it involves expanding $(x + h)^n$ for an arbitrary integer $n$, which is pretty tricky. It’s also worth pointing out that the theorem as stated only works for positive integer powers. We’ll later extend it to all nonzero numbers, but for now, we’ll have to stick with positive integers. !end-text-block !begin-example the derivative of $x^n$ Find $\frac{d}{dx}[x^6]$. To find this, we bring down the $6$ and decrease it by one into a $5$, to get $\frac{d}{dx}[x^6] = 6x^5$. !end-example !begin-exercise the derivative of $x^n$ Find $\frac{d}{dt}[t^3]$. !end-exercise !begin-text-block One of the most common mistakes made with derivatives is using the previous theorem on functions it doesn’t apply to. For example, although $(\cos(x))^5$ looks like it might have a derivative of $5(\cos(x))^4$, but the theorem only applies to $x^5$. Later on, we’ll see how to modify the derivative of $x^5$ to apply to similar-looking functions like this, but for now, we’re stuck with only functions of the form $x^n$. Next, let’s get the rest of the glue we need to get to polynomials — specifically, how do derivatives interact with addition, subtraction, and multiplication by constants? !end-text-block !begin-thm the sum, difference, and constant multiple rules Let $f$ and $g$ be differentiable functions and $c$ a constant number. 1. (The Sum Rule) $\frac{d}{dx}[f(x) + g(x)] = f'(x) + g'(x)$. 2. (The Difference Rule) $\frac{d}{dx}[f(x) - g(x)] = f'(x) - g'(x)$. 3. (The Constant Multiple Rule) $\frac{d}{dx}[c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot f'(x)$. !end-thm !begin-text-block These theorems tell us that derivatives split across addition and subtraction, and constant multiples factor out of derivatives. As an example, let’s revisit the intense second derivative question from the previous section. !end-text-block !begin-example the sum, difference, and constant multiple rules Find $\frac{d^2}{dx^2}[2x^2 - x]$. As before, we’ll handle one derivative at a time. $$ \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}[2x^2 - x] &= \frac{d}{dx}[2x^2] - \frac{d}{dx}[x]\\ &= 2\frac{d}{dx}[x^2] - \frac{d}{dx}[x]\\ &= 2(2x^1) - (1x^0)\\ &= 2(2x) - (1)\\ &= 4x - 1. \end{align*} $$ Now we need to find the derivative of $4x - 1$. $$ \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}[4x - 1] &= \frac{d}{dx}[4x] - \frac{d}{dx}[1]\\ &= 4\frac{d}{dx}[x] - \frac{d}{dx}[1]\\ &= 4(1) - 0\\ &= 4. \end{align*} $$ That was a whole lot easier than it was with the limit definition! !end-example !begin-exercise the sum, difference, and constant multiple rules Find $\frac{d}{dt}[t^2 - 5t + 2]$. !end-exercise !section !text s The Product and Quotient Rules
