!begin-text-block Now that we’ve talked about limits, let’s return to the idea we discussed in section 1: the slopes of curves. As a reminder, the slope of a curve at $x = a$ is the slope of the tangent line to the curve at $x = a$. In order to calculate this slope, we first find the slope of secant lines, which is just rise over run. If one point of the line is taken at $x = a$ and the other is taken at $x = a + h$, then the run is $h$ and the rise is $f(a + h) - f(a)$, so the slope is just $$ \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}. $$ !iframe https://www.desmos.com/calculator/lyfhz3puqb We want to get the tangent line by setting $h = 0$, but since the slope will be $\frac{0}{0}$ then, we need to take a limit instead. !end-text-block !begin-prop Let $f$ be a function that is continuous at $x = a$. Then the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at $x = a$ is $$ \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}. $$ !end-prop !begin-example slope of a graph Find the slope of the function $f(x) = x^2$ at $x = 1$. By the definition, we have that the slope is $$ \begin{align*} \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{f(1 + h) - f(1)}{h} &= \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{(1 + h)^2 - (1)^2}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{(1 + 2h + h^2) - 1}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{2h + h^2}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0^+} (2 + h). \end{align*} $$ That last line is justified, since we can cancel common factors in limits. Finishing up, we have $$ \begin{align*} \lim_{h \to 0^+} (2 + h) &= 2 + 0\\ &= 2. \end{align*} $$ This tells us that the slope of the graph (i.e. the slope of the tangent line) at $x = 1$ is $2$. !iframe https://www.desmos.com/calculator/jh1ausctpy !end-example !begin-exercise slope of a graph Find the slope of the function $g(x) = x^3$ at $x = -2$. !end-exercise !nav-buttons !footer
