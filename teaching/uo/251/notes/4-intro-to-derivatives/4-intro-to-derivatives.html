!begin-text-block Now that we’ve talked about limits, let’s return to the idea we discussed in section 1: the slopes of curves. As a reminder, the slope of a curve at $x = a$ is the slope of the tangent line to the curve at $x = a$. In order to calculate this slope, we first find the slope of secant lines, which is just rise over run. If one point of the line is taken at $x = a$ and the other is taken at $x = a + h$, then the run is $h$ and the rise is $f(a + h) - f(a)$, so the slope is just $$ \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}. $$ !iframe https://www.desmos.com/calculator/lyfhz3puqb We want to get the tangent line by setting $h = 0$, but since the slope will be $\frac{0}{0}$ then, we need to take a limit instead. !end-text-block !begin-prop Let $f$ be a function that is continuous at $x = a$. Then the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at $x = a$ is $$ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}. $$ !end-prop !begin-example slope of a graph Find the slope of the function $f(x) = x^2$ at $x = 1$. By the definition, we have that the slope is $$ \begin{align*} \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(1 + h) - f(1)}{h} &= \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{(1 + h)^2 - (1)^2}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(1 + 2h + h^2) - 1}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2h + h^2}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} (2 + h). \end{align*} $$ That last line is justified, since we can cancel common factors in limits. Finishing up, we have $$ \begin{align*} \lim_{h \to 0} (2 + h) &= 2 + 0\\ &= 2. \end{align*} $$ This tells us that the slope of the graph (i.e. the slope of the tangent line) at $x = 1$ is $2$. !iframe https://www.desmos.com/calculator/jh1ausctpy !end-example !begin-exercise slope of a graph Find the slope of the function $g(x) = x^3$ at $x = -2$. !end-exercise !begin-text-block We mentioned in section 1 that the slope of a tangent line measures rate of change of a function: for example, the rate of change of position is velocity. This idea is so important and used so often that we want to give it a name and simple notation, so that we don’t have to write down whole sentences to explain what we mean every time. !end-text-block !begin-def derivative Let $f$ be a function defined in an interval containing $x = a$, including at $a$ itself. The derivative of $f$ at $a$, written $f'(a)$ and read $f$ prime of $a$, is the slope of the tangent line to the graph at $x = a$. In other words, $$ f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}. $$ !end-def !begin-text-block We use the notation $f'(a)$ because the derivative is a whole new function: $f'(x)$ outputs the slope of the graph of $f$ at the point $x$. Taking a derivative is called differentiating, not deriving, for some reason. One more notational quirk: sometimes, we don’t want to give a function a name just so that we can put a prime on it to indicate that we want to take a derivative. In that case, we can just write $\frac{d}{dx}$ followed by the function in brackets: for example, $\frac{d}{dx}[x^2]$ means the derivative of $x^2$. !end-text-block !begin-example computing a derivative Find $\frac{d}{dx}[x^2]$. By definition, this is $$ \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2] &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h(2x + h)}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h)\\ &= 2x. \end{align*} $$ Therefore, $\frac{d}{dx}[x^2] = 2x$. !end-example !begin-exercise computing a derivative Find $\frac{d}{dx}[x^3]$. !end-exercise !nav-buttons !footer
