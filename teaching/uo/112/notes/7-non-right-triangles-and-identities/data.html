Section 7: Non-Right Triangles and Identities

With the ability to solve trig equations, we can approach another application of trig functions: their use in non-right triangles. Our ability to use trig functions in right triangles came from the unit circle, as with most things, and so to approach non-right triangles, we’ll place them in the unit circle too.

Right triangles have a lot of convenient structure that leads to straightforward terminology: once we’ve chosen an angle, we can label the sides as opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse. When there isn’t a right angle, though, things are a bit more complicated. Pictured here is a general non-right triangle with sides $a$, $b$, and $c$, and angles $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $\gamma$ (the letters $a$, $b$, and $c$ of the Greek alphabet). It feels like we might want to have those Greek letters touching the sides with the corresponding Latin letters, but there isn’t a good way to decide which of the two to pick. Instead, we’ll name things so that the sides are opposite their corresponding angles.

Let’s see what we can determine with this triangle. As we change $a$, $b$, and $\gamma$, the angles $\alpha$ and $\beta$ and the side length $c$ seem to be determined for us, meaning there should in theory be a way to find all three in terms of $a$, $b$, and $\gamma$.
